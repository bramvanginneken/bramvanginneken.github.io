I grew up in the south of the Netherlands, in <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuenen">Nuenen</a>, a village best known for the fact that Vincent van Gogh was unhappy there for a few years of his life. The second most famous person from Nuenen, following the wikipedia page, is a computer scientist! Edsger W. Dijkstra lived a few streets away from where I was born. My niece lived next to him for a while. Dijkstra's [Archive](https://www.cs.utexas.edu/users/EWD/) with a lot of handwritten manuscripts, even from after 2000, is amazing. 

After high school I decided to study physics at the nearby <a href="www.tue.nl">Eindhoven University of Technology</a>. I'm not really sure what made me do that, probably not the physics teacher. He was a friendly and very patient guy who tended to fill most of our classes staring out of the window for minutes in a row, while waiting for one of the less gifted pupils to answer a question. But I liked physics and at least I did not disagree with what my teachers told me about it, as I did with many of my English and French teachers who clearly had a different opinion about literature.  

Studying physics was quite interesting but the endless series of exams you had to pass became boring at some point and I decided to take a break and do my civil service (at that time every Dutch boy was still required to serve in the army, unless you refused and claimed to be a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conscientious_objector">conscientious objector</a> as I did successfully). I worked as a journalist for the university newspaper. Learning to write clearly with deadlines all the time is an extremely useful skill I picked up there. I continued to work as a free-lance journalist while continuing my Physics study at <a href="http://www.uu.nl">Utrecht University</a>. 

The courses I really liked during my first years were those about computers. We learned the basics of analog electronics, digital electronics, integrated circuits and CPUs, and we had to program these in machine language and, in a separate course, in <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbo_Pascal">Turbo Pascal</a>. Luckily, my father bought me a computer while I was a first year student. I spend days and nights programming the thing, and eventually I wrote a program that played <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connect_Four">Connect-4</a> about as good as I did. I did not know that at about the same time, that game <a href="http://homepages.cwi.nl/~tromp/c4/c4.html">was solved</a> by better computer programmers. Next I started to write a chess program, inspired by the thesis of <a href="http://www.cs.unimaas.nl/~herik/">Jaap van den Herik</a>, the first thesis I read in my life. It turned out to be a bit too complex for me to write a chess program that I could not easily beat, but it certainly hooked me on programming computers to do 'intelligent' things. 

By the time I was ready to do my final research project (equivalent to what you now have to do for a Master's Thesis) I had more or less decided that I'd get this over with quickly and I'd become a journalist. But then I started to investigate bricks. My research project involved the investigation of how materials reflect light. The idea was pretty simple: shoot light at pieces of bricks under various angles of illumination and measure how much light is reflected back in all possible directions.   I liked the fact it was such a concrete, visible thing. For some reason, everything with very small particles or very big invisible things like black holes, that should be the stuff that gets physicists all excited, never really worked for me. But the bricks did. I wrote a ray-tracer to do computer simulations of my reflection functions, automated the experimental set-up so I could do measurements day-and-night, almost burned down the lab because that lamp became very hot and the cardboard I had put around it too, and developed a new model using pieces of old models I read about in really old papers (papers that were written well before I was born, that you had to search in the damp basement of the Physics building). I liked doing research much more than I expected! Much of this had to do with the fact that the people I worked with (my supervisor, Maria Stavridi, a Greek postdoc, Astrid Kappers and Jan Koenderink) were genuinely interested in my work. This was very different from what I had experienced as a student so far; to most people working at a university students seemed to be creatures you subject to exercises, problems, and exams, and that you order to carry out so-called experiments where they do things that thousands of students have already done before them. I wrote <a href="http://www.isi.uu.nl/Research/Publications/publicationview.php?id=322">my first paper</a> about my model of how rough objects reflect light. I never worked harder on a paper than on that first one, carefully crafting every sentence. I thought it was brilliant, especially the confluent hypergeometric function that Mathematica told me I needed to normalize a distribution. A few years later someone sent me an e-mail with all sorts of difficult questions about the paper. We mailed back and forth and eventually he explained me how I could have done the mathematics in much simpler way. That confluent hypergeometric function was not needed at all...

I got my Physics degree and was offered to stay for one year at Physics of Man, continuing the work on reflection properties of materials. <a href="http://www1.cs.columbia.edu/~nayar/">Shree Nayar</a>, a professor of Columbia University who had just published <a href="http://www.sciencemag.org/cgi/content/abstract/267/5201/1153">a paper in Science</a> about a similar reflection model as the one I had proposed (so I was very impressed by him), visited and proposed to make a large database with many different types of objects photographed under a complete set of illumination and viewing angles. That turned out to be a brilliant idea. I worked hard on programming a crappy robot to orient pieces of wood with different materials glued on it in many directions, and on making a uniform light source together with the technicians of the group. <a href="http://www.ece.rutgers.edu/~kdana/Home.html">Kristin Dana</a>, a PhD student of Nayar, collected samples of all sort of stuff and came from New York to Utrecht, bringing an expensive high quality camera with her. For a few weeks we did measurements day and night on pieces of crumpled paper, aluminum foil, peacock feathers, good old bricks, lettuce and even a piece of skin from a corpse that we got from the pathology department at the university hospital. This resulted in the <a href="http://www1.cs.columbia.edu/CAVE//software/curet/">Columbia-Utrecht Reflectance and Texture Database</a>. Kristin wrote <a href="http://www.isi.uu.nl/Research/Publications/publicationview.php?id=145">a paper</a> about it and this is by far my most cited article (445 citations according to Google Scholar as of December 2007).

Finally I decided that I did *not* want to be a journalist, at least not for now. I saw an advertisement in the paper for a PhD position on programming a computer to detect tuberculosis in chest radiographs. 

To be continued ... This concludes the first 25 of my life. In the next episode, we'll cover the subsequent 25 years and see howe I ended up being a professor of medical image analysis at Radboud University Medical Center, in Nijmegen, a small town in the east of The Netherlands.
